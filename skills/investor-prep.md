COMPONENT 1: ONE-PAGER STRUCTURE

A one-pager is not a pitch deck summary. It is the single page an investor reads in 90 seconds to decide whether to take a meeting. It must answer six questions in order, and it must do so in plain language with no jargon.

Ask me about each section:

Section 1 — Problem
One to two sentences. What is broken, and who experiences it? No statistics yet. No solution. Just the problem stated plainly enough that a smart non-expert can understand it.
Push me if I lead with the solution, use technical jargon, or start with a market size. None of those belong here.

Section 2 — Solution
One to two sentences. What do you do, and how does it fix the problem stated above? The logic must be direct: "Our product does X, which means the person from Section 1 no longer experiences Y."
Flag it if the connection between problem and solution is unclear, if the solution description requires prior knowledge, or if I'm describing features rather than outcomes.

Section 3 — Market
Two numbers with methodology, not a paragraph. TAM and SAM, calculated bottoms-up, with the unit and price point shown explicitly. Do not accept a top-down market report citation as the market slide. If I don't have this worked out, tell me to use the Market Sizing skill first.

Section 4 — Traction
This is the most important section for pre-Series A companies. Traction is evidence that the world is responding to what you're building. Present it in order of strength:
- Revenue (ARR/MRR, growth rate, contract values)
- Paying customers (number, logo quality, retention/churn)
- Pipeline (qualified, not just "interested")
- Usage/engagement (DAU, WAU, activation rates, NPS)
- Non-revenue signals (LOIs, waitlist, pilot commitments, press)

If traction is thin, say so plainly — do not inflate or spin. Investors will find out. Instead, frame what the traction you do have proves about the hypothesis.

Section 5 — Team
Three to five sentences maximum. Cover: relevant domain expertise, prior startup or operator experience, any unfair advantages (proprietary technology, unique network, domain access). Do not list titles or bios — tell me why this team is the right team for this specific problem.

Section 6 — The Ask
State: the round size, the stage (pre-seed, seed, Series A), the primary use of proceeds (3 buckets), and the key milestones the raise will fund. Be specific: "We are raising $[X] to reach $[Y ARR] / hire [Z roles] / hit [specific product milestone] within [timeframe]."

After I fill in all six sections, synthesize them into a complete one-pager draft. Format it as clean prose, not bullet points. Then identify the two weakest sections and tell me specifically what would strengthen them.

COMPONENT 2: 30-SECOND NARRATIVE (THE COCKTAIL PARTY PITCH)

This is not a summary of your pitch deck. It is the conversational answer to "what do you do?" at a dinner party, on an airplane, or in an elevator with someone who has no context and no obligation to listen.

The cocktail party pitch has three parts and a trigger:

Part 1 — The Relatable Problem (10 seconds)
Start with something the listener can immediately visualize. Not "the SMB accounting software market is fragmented" — that is not relatable. "Every small business owner I've talked to dreads the last week of the quarter when they have to figure out their books" — that is relatable.

Part 2 — What You Do (10 seconds)
One sentence. What does the product do, stated simply enough that a curious non-expert would immediately understand it?

Part 3 — Why It's Working (10 seconds)
One signal that makes it credible. A customer number, a revenue figure, a retention rate, a well-known early customer, or a specific surprising data point. Not a claim — a fact.

The Trigger
End with a question or a hook that invites the listener to continue the conversation if they're interested, and lets them gracefully exit if they're not. "Are you seeing that in the companies you work with?" or "Happy to say more if that's interesting to you."

Draft the 30-second narrative for me based on what I share. Then test it: "If someone with no context heard this, what would their first question be?" Make sure the narrative answers that question preemptively.

COMPONENT 3: THE 20 HARDEST INVESTOR QUESTIONS

Ask me which round I am raising (pre-seed, seed, Series A). Then run me through the relevant questions for that stage. For each one, tell me what investors are actually trying to understand, and help me frame a strong answer.

PRE-SEED (idea to first customers) — Investors are primarily evaluating the team and the clarity of thinking.
1. Why are you the right person to solve this problem? (They're looking for domain expertise, personal connection to the problem, or unique access — not just passion.)
2. Who have you talked to? How many potential customers have you spoken with, and what did they tell you? (They're checking whether you've done primary research or are operating on assumptions.)
3. Why hasn't anyone solved this already? (They're testing whether you know the competitive landscape and can articulate a real gap.)
4. What's the simplest version of this you could build and sell in 90 days? (They're assessing whether you have product intuition and can scope.)
5. What would make you wrong? (They're testing self-awareness and intellectual honesty.)

SEED (product built, early customers) — Investors are evaluating whether the product is working and whether there's a real acquisition path.
6. What's your retention rate? (For SaaS: monthly/annual churn. For consumer: DAU/MAU or D1/D7/D30 retention. This is the single most important early signal of product-market fit.)
7. How did you get your first customers? (The answer reveals GTM instincts. "Through personal network" is honest but not scalable. "Through X specific channel at $Y CAC" is much better.)
8. What does your best customer look like, and how many of them are there? (ICP precision signal — and a test of whether you know who your real customer is yet.)
9. What have you learned that surprised you? (Tests learning agility and honesty — founders who say "nothing" aren't credible.)
10. Why is this the right time for this product to exist? (Timing question — what has changed in the last 2–3 years that makes this work now when it wouldn't have worked before?)
11. Walk me through your unit economics. (LTV, CAC, payback period, gross margin. Have these numbers ready and be able to derive them, not just recite them.)
12. What does the sales cycle look like, and what's your close rate? (For B2B — reveals GTM maturity and whether the conversion funnel is understood.)

SERIES A (initial traction, scaling readiness) — Investors are evaluating whether this is a repeatable, scalable business.
13. What's your growth rate, and what's driving it? (Not just the number — the decomposition. Is it new customers, expansion revenue, price increases? Is it sustainable?)
14. What's your net revenue retention? (For SaaS — NRR above 100% is a strong signal; below 90% is a serious concern.)
15. How do you define and measure product-market fit? (They want a specific metric, not a feeling. Cohort retention at a specific threshold, NPS, Sean Ellis score, etc.)
16. Why can't a well-funded competitor just copy this? (Defensibility question — moats, switching costs, data advantages, network effects, regulatory position.)
17. What does the org chart look like in 18 months? (GTM and product team scaling — reveals whether you understand what the next phase requires.)
18. What's the path to $50M ARR? (Requires a model: how many customers at what ACV, through what channels, with what team. Not a hockey stick graph — a bottoms-up argument.)
19. Who are the two or three investors in this round who are most likely to say yes, and why? (Social proof and deal dynamics — also tests whether you understand the investor landscape.)
20. What's the risk you're most worried about, and what are you doing about it? (The single most credible question to answer well — investors who hear an honest, specific answer trust founders more. Investors who hear "I don't see major risks" immediately lose confidence.)

For each question I ask you to prepare, do three things:
(1) Tell me what the investor is actually trying to understand
(2) Help me construct a strong answer using my actual data
(3) Flag any answer that is weak and tell me how to strengthen it before fundraising

COMPONENT 4: PITCH FRAMING AND RED FLAGS

STORY ARC
A pitch is not a data presentation — it is a story with a specific structure. Walk me through my pitch using this arc:

Act 1 — The World as It Is (the problem)
Set up the status quo. Make the investor feel the problem. Use a specific customer story or data point that makes the problem visceral, not statistical. The goal is for the investor to think "yes, I understand why this is broken."

Act 2 — The Insight (why this is possible now)
What do you know or believe that others don't? What has changed — in technology, behavior, regulation, or market structure — that makes this solution viable today? This is the "why now" moment. It should feel like an insight, not a market size slide.

Act 3 — Your Solution (what you built)
Show the product briefly. Lead with the outcome it produces, then explain how it works. Do not spend more than 20% of your pitch on the product — investors invest in markets and teams, not features.

Act 4 — Evidence (traction)
Show that the world is responding. The evidence should be the strongest possible signal you have — revenue first, then customers, then usage, then non-revenue signals. State growth rates in the most meaningful unit (week-over-week for very early stage, month-over-month for seed, quarter-over-quarter for Series A).

Act 5 — The Team (why you)
Cover domain expertise, relevant experience, and one unfair advantage. Keep it brief — 2–3 minutes in a verbal pitch, one slide in a deck. Do not read a bio.

Act 6 — The Ask (what you need and why)
State the amount, the lead you're hoping for or have, the timeline, and the specific milestones the capital will fund. Make it crisp — a vague ask signals uncertainty about the plan.

RED FLAGS INVESTORS LOOK FOR
Flag any of these in my pitch materials:

- Claiming "no competition" — this signals either poor market research or a non-existent market
- Market size in the first slide — leading with TAM before establishing the problem feels like a defense mechanism
- "We just need 1% of the market" — this is not a strategy, it is a math exercise that reveals no customer acquisition plan
- Traction presented as vanity metrics — downloads, signups, followers, or "interest from Fortune 500 companies" without conversion are not traction
- Revenue projections without bottom-up derivation — a hockey stick with no assumptions attached is not a financial model
- Team slides with titles but no relevant experience context — investors are evaluating why this team, not what their current titles are
- Not knowing your numbers — churn, CAC, LTV, gross margin should be available without hesitation; fumbling these signals loose operational control
- Asking for an NDA before sharing materials — this is a significant red flag to institutional investors and marks you as inexperienced

WHAT INVESTORS ACTUALLY CARE ABOUT BY STAGE

Pre-Seed: Team (70%) and insight clarity (30%). At this stage, there is rarely a product or customers — the investment is almost entirely a bet on the founders' ability to learn fast and execute. Investors are asking: "Would I want to work with this person for 7–10 years?"

Seed: Evidence of early pull (50%), team (30%), market size (20%). At seed, investors want to see that the world is responding. One or two early customers who are paying and staying is worth more than a sophisticated slide deck. They want to see that you can sell and that the product is working.

Series A: Unit economics and scalability (40%), growth rate (30%), market (20%), team (10%). By Series A, the question shifts from "is this working?" to "can this scale?" Investors are analyzing whether the business model is fundamentally sound at scale — not just whether it's growing, but whether it gets better or worse as it gets bigger.

## Framework & Methodology

### What Investors Are Actually Doing

Investors receive hundreds of pitches for every investment they make. Their primary job in the first meeting is not to be convinced — it is to find reasons to pass efficiently so they can protect their time for the opportunities that merit deeper diligence.

This inverts the intuition most founders bring to fundraising. Founders try to persuade. Investors try to falsify. The best investor preparation is not making the most optimistic possible case — it is anticipating the most credible objections and addressing them before they're raised.

The four components of this skill are designed around that asymmetry:

- The one-pager serves the investor's 90-second filtering process
- The 30-second narrative serves the informal, serendipitous introductions that often lead to meetings
- The Q&A preparation serves the diligence and objection-handling process
- The pitch framing and red flags serve the narrative coherence of a formal pitch

### The One-Pager: A Document That Earns the Meeting

The one-pager is often treated as a pitch deck extract. It is not. A pitch deck is a sequential visual argument designed for a live presentation where the founder controls the pacing. A one-pager is read asynchronously, in any order, by someone who has no obligation to continue reading.

This means the first two sentences of the one-pager carry disproportionate weight. Investors pattern-match immediately — they are looking for the problem statement that tells them whether they invest in this category and whether the founder understands the problem with the precision of someone who has spent time with customers. A vague or overly broad problem statement signals that the opportunity is not real or the founder hasn't done the work.

The traction section is the most important for pre-Series A companies because it is the section with the least room for narrative — it is evidence, not argument. A well-constructed traction section presents evidence in order of strength and frames it accurately: this is what we have, this is what it proves, this is what it doesn't prove. Investors appreciate honesty about what's missing more than inflated spin, because inflated spin is detectable and destroys trust in the rest of the document.

### The 30-Second Narrative: Credibility in Casual Contexts

The cocktail party pitch solves a specific problem: most investors first encounter a startup in an informal context where there is no presentation, no deck, and no formal attention. The quality of the impromptu verbal description often determines whether a formal meeting happens.

The three-part structure (relatable problem, what you do, why it's working) is designed around cognitive efficiency. A listener who doesn't know the space needs to build a mental model quickly. The problem statement gives them the world context. The solution statement tells them what changed. The proof point tells them whether to believe it. The trigger gives them a socially graceful way to engage further or exit.

The most common failure mode of the cocktail party pitch is leading with the solution ("we're an AI-powered platform for...") before establishing the problem. This forces the listener to backward-engineer the context and often produces a "I don't really understand what they do" reaction even after the conversation. Lead with the world as the customer experiences it, then introduce the solution.

### The 20 Questions: What's Actually Being Tested

Each of the 20 hard questions is probing a specific underlying concern. The structured approach of surfacing what the investor is actually trying to understand — before crafting the answer — is what separates prepared founders from founders who memorize responses without understanding the interrogation.

A few questions deserve special note:

**"What would make you wrong?"** (Q5) is the highest-signal question on intellectual honesty. The best founders can name specific scenarios — specific market conditions, specific competitive moves, specific customer behaviors — that would invalidate their core hypothesis. Founders who can't name these either haven't thought carefully about risk or are unwilling to share the vulnerabilities. Both are concerning signals.

**"What have you learned that surprised you?"** (Q9) tests learning agility. The expected answer from a founder who is iterating honestly includes at least one significant thing they believed at launch that turned out to be wrong. Founders who describe uninterrupted confirmation of their original hypothesis are either not learning or not sharing.

**"What's the risk you're most worried about?"** (Q20) is the closest investors can get to a lie detector. A specific, honest answer — "our biggest risk is that the enterprise sales cycle is longer than our runway, and we're currently managing that by targeting mid-market first" — builds more trust than any traction slide. It signals that the founder has done serious risk analysis and is managing risks rather than ignoring them.

### Red Flags: Why They Matter

The red flags list is derived from patterns that appear repeatedly in investor feedback. They are not arbitrary preferences — each one is a proxy for a deeper concern:

- "No competition" → either the market is too small, the founder hasn't researched it, or there's a reason incumbents haven't solved it that the founder should be able to explain
- "1% of the market" → signals no customer acquisition plan; a real sales strategy starts from customers, not market share percentages
- Vanity metrics → signals the team is optimizing for things that feel like progress but don't predict revenue

Understanding the underlying concern behind each flag allows founders to preemptively address them even when the surface-level mistake isn't present.

### Stage-Specific Investor Logic

The weighting of what investors care about by stage is not a soft observation — it reflects the actual due diligence checklists and decision criteria of institutional investors at each stage.

At pre-seed, the investment is literally a bet on the founders' ability to figure out something that hasn't been figured out yet. There is no product to analyze, no customers to interview, no revenue to model. The entire analysis is character and clarity of thinking.

At seed, there is usually a product and a handful of customers. The question shifts to: is the product creating value that customers will pay to keep? Early retention data — even with tiny sample sizes — is the most predictive signal at this stage. A seed-stage company with 10 customers and 90% retention is a more compelling investment than one with 50 customers and 60% retention.

At Series A, the analysis becomes financial. Investors are building models. They need to understand whether the unit economics improve at scale (does CAC drop as brand builds? does gross margin improve as infrastructure amortizes?), whether the market is large enough to support a $500M+ outcome at their ownership percentage, and whether the team can hire and manage the organization required to execute the scaling plan.

## Example Usage

**User:** I'm raising a $1.5M pre-seed for my compliance automation platform for fintech startups. We have a waitlist of 80 companies and 3 paid pilots at $500/month each. Former compliance officer at a bank, 2-person team.

**Claude:**

Good. Let's build your investor preparation. I'll start with the one-pager and work through to your most dangerous interview questions.

**COMPONENT 1: ONE-PAGER**

Let me ask about each section and draft as we go.

**Problem:** Fintech startups building on bank rails face an average of 14+ distinct compliance requirements from regulators, banking partners, and payment networks — each with different documentation formats and renewal timelines. Most early-stage fintechs have no compliance staff and founders with no prior compliance experience. The result is delayed launches, failed audits, and costly emergency consulting engagements.

*Is that accurate? Any adjustments?*

**Solution:** [Awaiting your input — what specifically does your platform do?]

*[User: "It automatically generates and maintains the compliance documentation fintech startups need — SOC 2 prep, BSA/AML policies, bank partner due diligence packages — and tracks renewal deadlines so nothing lapses."]*

Solution draft: We automate compliance documentation for fintech startups — generating, maintaining, and tracking the policy packages, audit prep materials, and bank partner due diligence documents that used to require expensive consultants or a full-time compliance officer.

**Market:** I'll need you to give me numbers here. How many fintech startups are founded each year in the US? What would a customer pay annually? Let's build this bottoms-up.

*[User: "Roughly 1,500–2,000 fintech startups funded each year in the US. We're targeting pre-Series B. Our target ACV is $12,000/year ($1,000/month)."]*

TAM: ~5,000 active pre-Series B US fintech startups × $12,000/year = $60M TAM (narrow but real)
SAM: companies that have already signed a bank partner agreement or are in the process — roughly 2,000 of the 5,000 × $12,000 = $24M SAM

This is a focused market, not a large one. At pre-seed, that's fine — but expect investors to ask about the expansion path (geographic expansion, adjacent verticals like insurtech or healthtech payments, moving up-market to Series B+).

**Traction:**
- 3 paid pilots at $500/month = $1,500 MRR ($18K ARR)
- 80-company waitlist
- Waitlist conversion to paid: 3/80 = 3.75% — flag: this number will be asked about; have an explanation for why 77 of 80 haven't converted yet (pricing, product stage, timing)

**Team:** You are a former compliance officer at a bank — this is directly relevant domain expertise and creates instant credibility with fintech founders who are afraid of compliance. Cover: years in compliance, specific institutions or regulatory bodies you've worked with, any specific notable experience (led a major audit, worked directly with OCC/FDIC, etc.). What's your co-founder's background?

**The Ask:** "We are raising $1.5M pre-seed to build out the full document automation engine (currently rule-based, moving to adaptive), hire one additional engineer, and grow from 3 to 25 paying customers within 12 months."

---

**COMPONENT 3: HARDEST PRE-SEED QUESTIONS FOR YOUR SITUATION**

**Q1 — Why are you the right person?**
What investors are testing: Is domain expertise real, or is "former compliance officer" a credential without depth?
Strong answer structure: "I spent X years at [Bank], where I personally built the compliance program for [specific product/program]. I've been on the side of the table reviewing fintech partner due diligence packages — I know exactly what banks look for and what kills deals. I've also been the person startups called when they failed their first audit. I've seen both failure modes from the inside."

**Q2 — Why hasn't this been solved?**
What investors are testing: Do you understand why incumbents haven't built this?
Strong answer: "Compliance consulting is a high-margin professional services business — the consultants have no incentive to productize their knowledge. The software that exists (Vanta, Drata) focuses on technical security compliance, not regulatory compliance specific to banking partnerships. That's a different problem, different documentation, different regulatory relationships."

**Q3 — What would make you wrong?**
Be honest here. Your most credible risks:
(1) Bank partners consolidate their due diligence requirements, making the documentation problem easier without a tool;
(2) Vanta or Drata decides to expand into bank-specific compliance documentation;
(3) The 80-company waitlist doesn't convert because the problem isn't painful enough to pay $1,000/month at the pre-seed stage.

Have a clear response to risk (3) — what does the low waitlist conversion rate tell you, and what are you doing about it?

**Investor Readiness Assessment:**

Strongest element: Founder-market fit is excellent. A compliance officer founding a compliance automation company has immediate credibility and prevents the most common early diligence question.

Biggest gap: The waitlist-to-paid conversion story. 80 waitlist companies but only 3 paying at half-price is a signal investors will probe. Before fundraising, either convert 5 more waitlist companies to full-price paying customers, or have a clear explanation for why the waitlist represents a different cohort (e.g., most joined before the product was live).

Recommended timeline: 60–90 days to close the conversion gap before going broad with investor outreach. Use this time to get to 8–10 paying customers at $1,000/month. That cohort, combined with your domain expertise, makes this a much more fundable seed.

## When to Use

- 4–8 weeks before you begin actively reaching out to investors — this is preparation time, not pitch time
- After a first investor meeting that didn't go well — debrief using the Q&A prep to diagnose which questions tripped you up
- When preparing to follow up with a warm investor introduction — the one-pager and 30-second narrative are what you send and say
- When joining an accelerator cohort — Demo Day preparation maps directly to the pitch framing component
- When a co-founder or team member needs to be able to speak to investors without you present — the playbook is a training document

## When NOT to Use

- As a substitute for having real traction — investor prep skills cannot fix a product or business that doesn't have evidence of working; they can only help you present what you have as clearly as possible
- To prepare for diligence conversations after a term sheet — the Q&A component covers early-stage interviews, not full legal and financial diligence processes
- To prepare for strategic partnerships or customer sales — the investor context (narrative arc, team credibility, market framing) is different from a customer sales context; don't use this framing to sell to customers
- When you are not yet sure what round you are raising — get clarity on your stage and ask first before running through the Q&A framework, which is stage-specific
