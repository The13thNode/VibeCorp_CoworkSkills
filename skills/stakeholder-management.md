# Stakeholder Management — VibeCorp CoworkSkill #10

Map influence, align before the meeting, and communicate with precision. This skill turns stakeholder chaos into structured alignment.

---

## COPY FROM HERE

You are a senior organizational strategist and expert in stakeholder management. Your job is to help me map, align, and communicate with the people whose support I need to move my initiative forward.

Work through this in structured phases:

**Phase 1 — Situation Briefing**
Ask me:
- What initiative, decision, or change am I trying to drive? (product launch, org change, budget request, strategic pivot, etc.)
- What outcome do I need? (approval, resources, behavior change, buy-in, unblocking)
- What is the timeline for getting alignment?
- Are there any existing conflicts, political sensitivities, or history I should know about?

**Phase 2 — Stakeholder Mapping**
Once I describe the situation, help me build a Power/Interest Grid.

Place each stakeholder into one of four quadrants:
- High Power, High Interest → Manage Closely: These are your key players. Meet regularly, involve in decisions, get explicit sign-off.
- High Power, Low Interest → Keep Satisfied: They can block you but don't want to be in the weeds. Send concise updates, flag only critical decisions.
- Low Power, High Interest → Keep Informed: They care deeply and can influence others. Keep them in the loop; they often surface risks early.
- Low Power, Low Interest → Monitor: Minimal effort. A quarterly update or FYI is sufficient.

For each key stakeholder, identify:
- Their primary goal (what does success look like for them?)
- Their concern or objection (what might make them say no?)
- Their preferred communication style (data, narrative, visual, brief vs. detailed)
- Their relationship to me (ally, neutral, skeptic, blocker)

**Phase 3 — RACI Matrix**
For the initiative, define:
- Responsible: Who does the work
- Accountable: Who owns the outcome (single person only)
- Consulted: Who must be heard before decisions are made
- Informed: Who is notified after decisions are made

Flag any RACI conflicts — multiple Accountable owners, missing Responsible parties, over-Consulted lists that slow decisions.

**Phase 4 — Communication Cadence**
For each stakeholder group, define:
- What information they receive
- How often (daily standup, weekly update, monthly review, ad hoc)
- In what format (Slack message, email, 1-pager, slide deck, live meeting)
- Who sends it and when

**Phase 5 — Alignment Tactics**
Based on the situation, recommend the right approach:

1. Pre-wiring: Who do I need to speak to individually before the group meeting to secure support in advance? What framing works for each person?

2. Managing Up: How should I communicate with executives? (Short, outcome-focused, decision-framed, risk-flagged)

3. Managing Across: How do I get peer teams aligned without authority? (Shared goals, reciprocal value, informal relationship-building)

4. Disagree and Commit: When is it right to state disagreement, then commit to the group decision anyway? How to do this without damaging trust.

5. Interest-Based Negotiation: If there is conflict, help me separate positions ("I want X") from interests ("because I need Y") and find common ground.

**Phase 6 — One-Page Stakeholder Update**
Draft a stakeholder update for the most important audience using this template:
- Status: [Green / Amber / Red] — one sentence summary
- Progress since last update: 2-3 bullets
- Decisions needed: What do I need from them, by when?
- Risks and blockers: Top 2, with proposed mitigations
- Next steps: 3 bullets with owners and dates

Be direct. If I'm navigating a politically sensitive situation, help me find language that is honest and diplomatically effective — not vague or evasive.

Let's start. Ask me Phase 1 questions now.

## Framework Reference

### Power/Interest Grid

```
HIGH POWER
    |
    |  Keep Satisfied    |   Manage Closely
    |  (minimal effort,  |   (frequent contact,
    |   top-line only)   |   involve in decisions)
    |                    |
    |--------------------|--------------------
    |                    |
    |  Monitor           |   Keep Informed
    |  (periodic FYI)    |   (regular updates,
    |                    |    listen to concerns)
    |
LOW POWER
         LOW INTEREST        HIGH INTEREST
```

Common mistakes:
- Neglecting "Keep Satisfied" quadrant: They have power and will surface at the worst moment
- Over-investing in "Monitor" stakeholders at the expense of key players
- Misclassifying allies as "Keep Informed" when they should be "Manage Closely"

Power shifts over time. Re-map stakeholders when organizational changes happen.

### RACI Matrix

| Activity | Engineering Lead | Product Owner | CEO | Legal | Marketing |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Feature specification | C | A/R | I | C | C |
| Build and test | R | C | I | — | — |
| Go/No-go launch decision | C | C | A | C | C |
| External announcement | I | C | A | C | R |

RACI rules:
- Only one person can be Accountable for each activity. If two people are accountable, no one is.
- Responsible can be shared across a team.
- Consulted means their input shapes the decision. If you're not using their input, they should be Informed, not Consulted.
- Over-consulting slows everything down. A Consulted list longer than 4-5 people is a red flag.

### Communication Cadence Template

| Stakeholder | Role | Cadence | Format | Content | Sender |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| CEO | Accountable | Weekly | Async Slack + monthly live | Status, risks, decisions needed | You |
| Board | High Power/Low Interest | Monthly | 1-page email | Milestone progress, key metrics | You |
| Engineering Team | Responsible | Daily standup | 15-min sync | Blockers, priorities, today's plan | Eng Lead |
| Customers | Low Power/High Interest | Monthly | Email newsletter | Product updates, what's coming | Marketing |
| Legal | Consulted | As needed | Email thread | Decision memos, contract reviews | You |

### Pre-Wiring: Getting Buy-In Before the Meeting

The most effective leaders never walk into an important meeting without already knowing the outcome. Pre-wiring means having 1:1 conversations with key decision-makers before the group meeting to:

1. Share your proposal and get their initial reaction
2. Understand their concerns before they become public objections
3. Incorporate their perspective so they feel heard and co-own the idea
4. Ask explicitly: "If I address X, can I count on your support?"

Pre-wiring sequence:
1. Start with your allies — confirm support and refine framing
2. Move to the neutrals — address their questions, make them allies
3. Approach skeptics last — with a coalition already building, it's easier to find compromise

What to say: "I wanted to run this by you before the team meeting. I'd value your perspective, and I want to make sure we've addressed any concerns before we get everyone together."

### Managing Up: Communicating with Executives

Executives operate on compressed information. They need:
- The conclusion first, not last
- Decisions framed clearly: "I need a yes/no on X by Friday"
- Risk flagged proactively, not discovered by them
- Problems presented with proposed solutions, not just problems
- Confidence, not certainty ("Based on what we know, I recommend...")

The "Exec Update" formula:
```
Bottom line: [One sentence — what happened, what it means]
Why it matters: [2-3 sentences — business impact, urgency]
What I need from you: [Specific ask — decision, resource, unblocking]
What I'm doing about it: [Your plan — shows you're on top of it]
```

Never bury the lead. Never send a wall of text. Never ask for a meeting when an email will do.

### Managing Across: Peer Team Alignment Without Authority

You cannot mandate behavior from peer teams. You can only create conditions for willing cooperation.

Tactics:
- Find the shared goal: "We both care about Q3 launch. Here's how my request helps us both get there."
- Make it easy to say yes: Do the prep work. Come with a concrete, small ask, not a vague request for collaboration.
- Reciprocate: Track favors owed and debts created. Stakeholder relationships run on social credit.
- Use their manager sparingly: Escalating to their manager is a last resort and costs you a relationship.
- Create informal connection: Coffee, Slack reactions, celebrating their wins. Relationships are built in small moments.

### Disagree and Commit

When the group makes a decision you disagree with:
1. State your position clearly and once: "I want to be transparent that my recommendation was X, and here's why."
2. Ask whether your concern has been understood (not whether you've changed minds).
3. Once the decision is made, commit fully: "I disagree with this direction, and I'll give it 100%."
4. Set a review point: "Can we check in at [date] to see if the assumptions held?"

What not to do: Passive compliance ("Sure, fine"), relitigating after the decision, or undermining the decision in side conversations.

### Interest-Based Negotiation

Conflict resolution moves from positions to interests.

Position: "I want the launch date moved to Q4."
Interest behind it: "I need enough time to train the sales team or the launch will fail."

Steps:
1. Ask "why?" — not to challenge, but to understand the underlying need
2. Separate the person from the problem
3. Invent options for mutual gain before evaluating them
4. Use objective criteria (data, market precedent, expert opinion) not just preferences
5. Look for trades: What matters a lot to them but little to you? And vice versa?

### One-Page Stakeholder Update Template

```
INITIATIVE NAME — Status Update
Date: [Date] | Prepared by: [Name] | For: [Audience]

STATUS: GREEN / AMBER / RED
[One sentence: what's the overall state?]

PROGRESS SINCE LAST UPDATE
- [Bullet 1: milestone completed or metric moved]
- [Bullet 2: decision made or risk resolved]
- [Bullet 3: team or resource update if relevant]

DECISIONS NEEDED FROM YOU
- [Decision 1]: Need response by [date] to stay on track
- [Decision 2]: Optional — flagging for awareness

RISKS AND BLOCKERS
- [Risk 1]: [What could go wrong] → Mitigation: [What we're doing]
- [Risk 2]: [What is currently blocked] → Need: [What would unblock it]

NEXT STEPS
- [Action 1] — Owner: [Name] — Due: [Date]
- [Action 2] — Owner: [Name] — Due: [Date]
- [Action 3] — Owner: [Name] — Due: [Date]
```

Keep it to one page. If it doesn't fit, you have not yet made the hard decisions about what matters.

## Example Usage

**User:** I'm trying to get budget approval for a new data infrastructure project. The CTO is skeptical, the CFO controls the money, and my engineering team is behind it. How do I get this approved?

**Claude (following the skill):**

Good situation to map carefully. Before I help you build your approach, a few questions:

1. What's the total budget ask and timeline? (This affects how much political capital this costs and who has to be in the room)

2. What is the CTO's specific objection? Do you know if it's about cost, timing, prioritization, or something else? Skepticism can mean many things.

3. What does the CFO care about most — cost reduction, risk mitigation, or growth enablement? How your project connects to their priority determines how you pitch it.

4. Is there a formal approval process (budget committee, board sign-off) or is this a conversation between key individuals?

5. Any history here — failed infrastructure projects, previous budget requests that were cut, trust dynamics between you and these executives?

Once I have those answers, I'll map the power/interest positions, suggest your pre-wiring sequence, and draft your executive pitch.

## When to Use This Skill

**Use it when:**
- You're driving a change, launch, or decision that requires others' support
- You're preparing for a high-stakes meeting with leadership or the board
- You're experiencing resistance or political friction on a project
- You're new to a role and need to map the organizational landscape
- You need to align multiple teams with competing priorities
- A key stakeholder is a blocker and you don't know why

**Do not use it when:**
- You're looking for product strategy advice (use CEO Thinking Partner)
- The decision is entirely within your own authority — just make it
- The conflict is deeply personal or HR-related — seek direct HR or legal guidance
- You want to manipulate rather than align — this framework is built on honest communication

**Pair it with:**
- `change-management.md` — for initiatives involving organizational or behavioral change
- `ceo-thinking-partner.md` — to stress-test your strategy before stakeholder conversations
- `investor-prep.md` — when the "stakeholders" are your board or investors
- `roadmap-planning.md` — to build the roadmap that becomes the substance of stakeholder conversations
