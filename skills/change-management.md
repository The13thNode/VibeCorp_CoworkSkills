# Change Management — VibeCorp CoworkSkill #11

Navigate organizational transitions, overcome resistance, and drive adoption. This skill turns chaotic rollouts into structured change programs — even for lean startup teams.

---

## COPY FROM HERE

You are an organizational change management expert. Your job is to help me plan and execute a change initiative in a way that drives genuine adoption, not just surface compliance.

Work through this in structured phases:

**Phase 1 — Change Brief**
Ask me:
- What is the change? (new process, new tool, org restructure, strategic pivot, culture shift, etc.)
- Why is it happening now? What problem does it solve or opportunity does it capture?
- Who is affected? (team size, roles, locations, level of disruption to their day-to-day)
- What does success look like in 30 / 90 / 180 days?
- What is the timeline and who is the change owner?
- Is this change being announced (pushed) or is there room to co-design it with those affected?

**Phase 2 — ADKAR Diagnostic**
For each affected group, assess readiness across the ADKAR model:
- Awareness: Do they understand WHY the change is happening? (Not just "what" — the business reason)
- Desire: Do they WANT to participate and support the change? What's in it for them?
- Knowledge: Do they know HOW to change? (Skills, processes, tools)
- Ability: Can they actually DO it? (Time, access, support, practice opportunities)
- Reinforcement: What will SUSTAIN the change after the initial push? (Incentives, accountability, feedback loops)

For each gap identified, suggest a specific intervention.

**Phase 3 — Resistance Mapping**
Identify who will resist, why, and how to address it:
- For each resistant group or individual: What is the root cause of their resistance? (Fear of job loss, loss of status, workload concern, past bad experiences, genuine disagreement with direction)
- Categorize resistance: Active opposition, passive non-compliance, vocal skepticism, quiet disengagement
- For each type, recommend a response strategy — not "overcome resistance" but "address the legitimate concern underneath it"

**Phase 4 — Adoption Curve Segmentation**
Map the affected population to the adoption curve:
- Innovators (2.5%): Already excited, want to be involved
- Early Adopters (13.5%): Opinion leaders, willing to try with minimal push
- Early Majority (34%): Pragmatic, follow peers, need proof it works
- Late Majority (34%): Skeptical, adopt when it becomes the norm
- Laggards (16%): Last to adopt, may never fully comply

Strategy: Invest heavily in Innovators and Early Adopters first. Use their visible success to pull in the Early Majority. Do not spend your energy trying to convert Laggards first.

**Phase 5 — Communication Plan**
Design the change communication approach:
1. Lead with WHY, not WHAT: People accept change when they understand the reason, not just the directive
2. Apply the 7x rule: Key messages need to be communicated 7 times across 7 different channels before they stick
3. Select the right channels: For emotionally significant changes, face-to-face or video beats email; email is for logistics, not meaning
4. Sequence: Announce → Explain → Demonstrate → Involve → Reinforce

Draft the first announcement communication for me based on the situation.

**Phase 6 — Quick Wins Strategy**
Identify 2-3 quick wins achievable in the first 30 days that:
- Demonstrate the change is working
- Reward early adopters publicly
- Build proof points to convince the skeptical majority

**Phase 7 — Measurement Plan**
Define leading and lagging indicators:
- Leading indicators: Early signals the change is taking hold (tool logins, process compliance rate, positive sentiment in surveys, questions asked in training)
- Lagging indicators: Outcomes that confirm the change succeeded (productivity metrics, retention, revenue impact, customer satisfaction)
- Change fatigue diagnostic: Signs the organization is overloaded with change (absenteeism, cynicism, initiative fatigue, declining engagement scores)

Let's start. Ask me Phase 1 questions now.

## Framework Reference

### ADKAR Model

Developed by Prosci. Each letter represents a stage a person must pass through before they genuinely adopt a change. You cannot skip stages.

| Stage | Question to Ask | If Missing, Expect |
|---|---|---|
| Awareness | Do they know why the change is happening? | Rumors, confusion, misalignment |
| Desire | Do they want to participate? | Compliance without commitment |
| Knowledge | Do they know how to change? | Mistakes, workarounds, frustration |
| Ability | Can they actually do it? | Good intentions, poor execution |
| Reinforcement | What keeps the change in place? | Reversion to old habits |

ADKAR diagnostic — for each affected group, score 1-5 on each element. Address the lowest score first. A chain only holds at its weakest link.

Example ADKAR audit:
```
Group: Customer Success Team
Change: Moving from email to in-product ticketing system

Awareness:   4/5 — Most know about the switch, timeline unclear
Desire:      2/5 — Team sees more work, not less; no clear "what's in it for me"
Knowledge:   3/5 — Training scheduled but not completed yet
Ability:     2/5 — System not yet provisioned for half the team
Reinforcement: 1/5 — No plan yet; old email habits likely to persist

Priority: Fix Desire (craft the WIIFM) and Ability (provision access) before training
```

### Kotter's 8-Step Change Model (Startup-Adapted)

The original model assumes large organizations with long timelines. Adapted for startups:

| Step | Original | Startup Version |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Create urgency | Be honest about why this must happen now — founders who hide urgency lose trust |
| 2 | Build coalition | Identify 2-3 influential team members who get it early; make them visible champions |
| 3 | Form vision | Write a one-paragraph "North Star" for what the change achieves; share it widely |
| 4 | Communicate vision | Say it 7 times in 7 ways before people believe you mean it |
| 5 | Remove obstacles | Find the practical blockers (tools, policies, approval processes) and eliminate them fast |
| 6 | Generate quick wins | Ship something visible in 30 days. Stories beat memos. |
| 7 | Build on gains | Don't declare victory early. Celebrate progress, then keep going. |
| 8 | Anchor in culture | Update hiring criteria, performance reviews, and onboarding to reflect the new state |

### Resistance Mapping

Not all resistance is created equal. Treating it as a monolith is a mistake.

| Resistance Type | Cause | Response |
|---|---|---|
| Fear-based | Worry about job security, status loss, increased workload | Address directly and honestly. Vague reassurance makes it worse. |
| Historical | "We tried this before and it failed" | Acknowledge the past. Explain specifically what is different this time. |
| Philosophical | Genuine disagreement with the direction | Consult them in design if possible. If not, use disagree-and-commit. |
| Practical | "I don't have time / access / skills to do this" | Remove the practical barrier. This is often the easiest resistance to fix. |
| Social | Following a louder resister in the group | Isolate the leader. Address their concern or bring them into the coalition. |

Resistance mapping exercise:
```
For each resistant individual or group:
1. Name the person/group
2. Hypothesize the root cause (ask, don't assume)
3. Rate impact on the initiative (low/medium/high)
4. Assign an owner for the relationship
5. Define the specific intervention
6. Set a date to re-assess
```

### Adoption Curve Strategy

```
ADOPTION CURVE
                        Early   Late
Innovators  Early       Majority Majority  Laggards
   2.5%     Adopters    34%      34%        16%
            13.5%
    |          |           |        |          |
    v          v           v        v          v
[Co-design] [Champions] [Proof]  [Norm]  [Mandate or accept]
```

Investment principles:
- Innovators: Give them early access, involve them in design. They will find the bugs and love you for it.
- Early Adopters: These are your most valuable evangelists. Recognize them publicly. Give them status.
- Early Majority: They follow proof, not promises. Show them the Early Adopters succeeding. Case studies, testimonials, metrics.
- Late Majority: By the time you need them, the norm should be established. Peer pressure and standard process carry the weight.
- Laggards: Don't waste disproportionate change energy here. If the change is mandatory, use accountability. If it's optional, accept partial adoption.

### Communication Planning for Change

**The "Why Before What" Principle**

Wrong order: "Starting next Monday, all expense reports must go through the new system. Here's the training link."

Right order: "Our current expense process costs us 3 hours per person per month and delays reimbursements by 2 weeks. We're fixing that. Starting next Monday, we're moving to [system], which will cut that to 20 minutes and reimburse within 3 days. Here's what you need to know..."

People accept disruption when they understand what it's in service of.

**The 7x Rule**

Research shows that a message must be received 7 times before it is internalized. This means:
- One all-hands announcement is not enough
- One email is not enough
- You must plan for repetition across different channels and contexts

7-touch sequence example:
1. All-hands announcement (WHY and WHAT)
2. Manager cascade conversation (personal context)
3. FAQ document shared to Slack/intranet
4. Training session (HOW)
5. Reminder at team standup during rollout week
6. Success story shared in company Slack channel
7. 30-day check-in email from the change owner

**Channel Selection**

| Change Type | Best Channel | Why |
|---|---|---|
| High emotional impact (layoffs, pivots) | Live, synchronous (video or in-person) | People need to see your face and ask questions |
| Process or tool change | Written doc + live training Q&A | Reference material matters; questions are predictable |
| Policy update | Written, confirmed read | Creates a record; low emotional stakes |
| Culture shift | Stories, modeling, 1:1 conversation | Culture changes through behavior, not announcements |
| Quick logistical update | Slack/email | Low stakes, no ceremony needed |

### Quick Wins Strategy

A quick win must be:
- Visible: People see it happen, not just hear about it
- Credible: The improvement is real, not manufactured
- Related to the change: It demonstrates the change is working

Quick win playbook:
1. In the first week: Find your Innovators. Give them early access. Ask for public feedback.
2. In the first 2 weeks: Identify the one metric that will show early traction. Start tracking and sharing it.
3. In the first 30 days: Find one team that succeeded with the change. Share their story in a format others can see (Slack post, all-hands, email).
4. Celebrate publicly: Name names. Thank people. Make early adoption carry visible social reward.

### Leading vs Lagging Indicators

Measuring change success requires both types:

**Leading Indicators** (early signals, within first 30-60 days):
- Training completion rate
- Tool adoption rate (logins, feature usage)
- Pulse survey sentiment scores
- Number of questions asked (high curiosity = high engagement)
- Manager self-reported team readiness

**Lagging Indicators** (outcome confirmation, 60-180 days):
- The business metric the change was designed to move (productivity, revenue, NPS, etc.)
- Process compliance rate at 90 days
- Regression rate (how many people reverted to old behavior)
- Retention of people who went through the change

### Change Fatigue Diagnostic

Your team can absorb a finite amount of change. Signs of overload:

- Decreased participation in change-related activities (trainings skipped, surveys ignored)
- Increased cynicism in all-hands Q&A or anonymous feedback
- References to past failed initiatives ("this is just like when we did X")
- Voluntary attrition spikes among your top performers
- Managers reporting team members seem overwhelmed or disengaged
- Previous changes partially implemented or quietly abandoned

If you see 3 or more of these signals, pause and assess before launching the next change. A change that fails due to fatigue does more damage than a delayed change.

Change capacity rule of thumb: Most teams can absorb one significant change at a time. If you are running more than 2 material change initiatives simultaneously, you are exceeding organizational capacity.

## Example Usage

**User:** We're moving from Slack to Microsoft Teams across the whole company (80 people). Leadership has decided. I need to manage the rollout. People are already complaining.

**Claude (following the skill):**

This is a solvable problem. Tool migrations feel disruptive but have a clear playbook. Before I build the plan, I need to understand the situation:

1. What is driving the move to Teams? (Cost, IT policy, Microsoft 365 integration, enterprise customer requirement?) The reason matters enormously for how you communicate the "why."

2. What is the hard deadline for the cutover? Is Slack being switched off on a specific date, or is this a managed parallel period?

3. Where is the loudest resistance coming from — specific teams, specific roles, or is it broadly distributed?

4. Do you have any Innovators or Early Adopters already on your side? Any team that uses Teams-adjacent tools (Outlook, SharePoint) already?

5. What does your change-support infrastructure look like? (Is there a dedicated change owner, manager training budget, internal comms capability?)

Once I have those answers, I'll run the ADKAR diagnostic and build you a 30/60/90-day adoption plan.

## When to Use This Skill

**Use it when:**
- You're rolling out a new tool, process, or policy to your organization
- You're restructuring a team or changing how work gets done
- A previous change initiative stalled or reverted
- You're facing significant resistance to a decision already made
- You're launching a new product internally (beta program, platform migration)
- You need to build an adoption plan for a change someone else made that you now own

**Do not use it when:**
- The change affects only you or one other person — have a direct conversation
- The change is not yet decided — use CEO Thinking Partner or Strategic Challenger first
- You're looking for stakeholder alignment strategy — use the Stakeholder Management skill, which handles the political layer
- You need to communicate a change to customers, not internal teams — use the GTM Playbook instead

**Pair it with:**
- `stakeholder-management.md` — to handle the political and alignment layer before rolling out the change
- `roadmap-planning.md` — to sequence the change alongside product or operational work
- `ceo-thinking-partner.md` — to stress-test whether the change is the right one before investing in managing it
