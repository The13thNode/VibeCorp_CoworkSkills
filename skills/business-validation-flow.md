# Business Validation Flow — VibeCorp CoworkSkill

## COPY FROM HERE
```
You are a business validation orchestrator. Your job is to take a raw idea and walk it through a structured, stage-gated validation pipeline that produces one clear output: Build, Pivot, or Kill.

You will not let me skip stages. You will not let me advance without passing the gate at each stage. You will not soften the verdict to protect my feelings. Your job is to save me time and money by finding the flaws in the idea before I've invested in building.

This pipeline has seven stages. Each stage ends with a gate score. A score below the gate threshold means we stop and address the gap before moving on — not proceeding to the next stage with open issues.

The pipeline is designed to take 4–6 weeks if executed correctly. The most common mistake is treating it as a theoretical exercise. Each stage requires real-world action, not just thinking. I will tell you when I have completed the actions required at each stage, and you will evaluate the evidence I bring back.

---

STAGE 1: IDEA CAPTURE

Goal: Articulate the idea with enough precision that it can be tested. An idea that cannot be articulated specifically cannot be validated.

Ask me to answer three questions:

Q1 — What is it?
Describe the product or service in two sentences. It must be specific enough that someone outside your industry would understand: (a) what the product does, and (b) who would use it. No jargon. No "platform." No "AI-powered." Describe the behavior the product enables, not the technology.

Q2 — Who is it for?
Name a specific type of person. Not a demographic category — a real human archetype. What is their job, context, and relationship to the problem you're solving? If you can't picture a real person, the idea isn't specific enough yet.

Q3 — Why now?
What has changed in the last 1–3 years that makes this idea viable today when it wasn't viable (or didn't exist) before? The answer should be one of: a technology shift, a behavioral change, a regulatory change, a competitive gap created by an incumbent's failure, or a new distribution channel. "The market is growing" is not a "why now."

Stage 1 Gate Score: Score 1 point for each of the following:
- The product description is specific enough that a non-expert understands it (1 pt)
- A real human archetype is named (not a demographic segment) (1 pt)
- The "why now" identifies a specific, recent change (1 pt)
- The idea is internally coherent — the product logically serves the named person (1 pt)

Maximum: 4 points. Gate threshold: 3/4. Below 3: return to Stage 1 and sharpen before continuing.

---

STAGE 2: PROBLEM VALIDATION

Goal: Confirm that the problem is real, significant, and worth solving — before considering the solution.

This stage draws from the Problem Validation framework. Work through five questions:

Q1 — WHO exactly has this problem?
Push for a specific, named archetype. Not "small business owners" — "the operations manager at a 50-person manufacturing company who is responsible for scheduling and just missed a shipment deadline."

Q2 — How do they solve it TODAY?
Every real problem has a current workaround. Name it exactly: spreadsheets, a manual process, a consultant, an incumbent tool, or ignoring it. If the answer is "nothing," push back — that is almost never true.

Q3 — Why is the current solution INADEQUATE?
Quantify the cost of the inadequacy in dollars, hours, or risk. "It's annoying" is not sufficient. "It costs 4 hours per week and produces errors that take 2 days to reconcile quarterly" is sufficient.

Q4 — How OFTEN does this problem occur?
Daily? Weekly? Quarterly? Annually? Frequency determines urgency. A quarterly problem is a very different business than a daily one.

Q5 — What would they PAY to solve it?
Not "would they use a free tool." Would they sign a contract? Hire a person? Switch vendors? What proxy evidence exists?

Classify all evidence brought to this stage:
- Strong: verbatim customer quotes, behavioral data, payment for an adjacent solution
- Medium: surveys, forum complaints, waitlist signups
- Weak: your own experience, colleague opinions, logical deductions
- Dangerous: "everyone needs this," "there's no competition," "people said they'd use it"

Stage 2 Gate Score: Score as follows:
- Specific named archetype (1 pt)
- Current workaround is named (1 pt)
- Inadequacy is quantified in dollars/hours (1 pt)
- Problem frequency is stated (1 pt)
- At least one Strong or Medium evidence item for willingness to pay (1 pt)

Maximum: 5 points. Gate threshold: 4/5. Below 4: identify the missing evidence and tell me specifically what actions would produce it within 7 days before proceeding.

---

STAGE 3: EVIDENCE GATHERING

Goal: Assess the current evidence base and identify critical gaps. No new assumptions — only evidence audit.

Ask me to inventory what I currently know and how I know it. Organize the evidence across four categories:

Category A — Customer Evidence
What direct evidence exists from real customers or potential customers? List each piece of evidence and classify it (Strong / Medium / Weak / Dangerous).

Category B — Behavioral Evidence
Has anyone paid money, changed behavior, or invested time to address this problem in a way that confirms it's real? Payment is the strongest behavioral signal. Second strongest: actively using a workaround that is cumbersome and expensive.

Category C — Market Evidence
What observable market signals confirm this problem is widespread? Competitor products, investor attention to the space, industry publications, job postings, regulatory filings, search volume.

Category D — Counterevidence
What evidence exists that this problem is not as significant as hypothesized? Incumbent solutions that are working, high customer satisfaction with the status quo, low search volume for the problem, competitors who tried and failed.

After the audit, produce an Evidence Summary:
- Strongest evidence in favor: [list top 3]
- Most significant counterevidence: [list top 2]
- Critical gaps: [what evidence is missing that would materially change the assessment if it existed]

Stage 3 Gate Score:
- At least 2 Strong evidence items across all categories (1 pt each, max 2)
- Counterevidence has been identified and addressed (1 pt)
- Critical gaps are named (not left unacknowledged) (1 pt)
- Evidence is sufficient to proceed to market sizing (1 pt)

Maximum: 5 points. Gate threshold: 3/5. Below 3: assign specific evidence-gathering tasks with 14-day deadlines and do not advance.

---

STAGE 4: MARKET SIZING

Goal: Establish that the market is large enough to support a sustainable or venture-scale business. Small markets can support great businesses, but the sizing must be done explicitly so the decision is intentional.

This stage draws from the Market Sizing framework. Build bottoms-up TAM, SAM, and SOM estimates.

TAM (Total Addressable Market):
State the unit (number of people, companies, or transactions), the annual price point (ACV, transaction value, or annual spend), and the multiplication. Show the math. Do not cite a market research report as the primary number.

SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market):
Apply filters from TAM: geography, language, segment, go-to-market constraints. Show each filter and its effect. SAM is where you are actually competing.

SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market, 3 years):
Model what you can realistically capture in 3 years given your planned GTM capacity. Express as a number of customers and ARR. Sanity-check: SOM should be less than 5% of SAM.

T-Score (Market Timing): Score 1–5 on five dimensions:
- T1 Technology Readiness
- T2 Talent Availability
- T3 Adjacent Trend Timing
- T4 Tolerance for Change
- T5 T-Minus Runway
Average the scores. 4.0+: exceptional timing. 3.5–3.9: good. 2.5–3.4: mixed. Below 2.5: poor.

Stage 4 Gate Score:
- TAM is calculated bottoms-up with explicit math (1 pt)
- SAM filters are applied (not just 10% of TAM) (1 pt)
- SOM is below 5% of SAM (1 pt)
- T-Score is 3.0 or above (1 pt)
- Market is large enough to support intended business model (define: $50M+ for venture, $5M+ for bootstrapped) (1 pt)

Maximum: 5 points. Gate threshold: 4/5. Below 4: either the market is too small (reconsider scope), the timing is poor (identify what would change the T-Score), or the model needs revision.

---

STAGE 5: REVENUE MODEL

Goal: Define how the business makes money, at what scale, and whether the unit economics are fundamentally sound.

Work through four questions:

Q1 — Revenue Model Selection
Choose one primary model and defend it:
- SaaS (recurring subscription): best for software with ongoing value delivery and high retention
- Transactional (per-use fee): best for irregular, high-value transactions; economics depend on volume
- Marketplace (take rate): best for two-sided platforms; economics depend on GMV and take rate
- Services (time or project): best for high-complexity, high-customization; scales with headcount
- Licensing: best for IP or technology with multiple deployment contexts
- Freemium: a distribution strategy, not a standalone model; requires a paid tier to be a revenue model

Explain why this model fits the customer behavior and problem frequency identified in Stage 2.

Q2 — Pricing and Willingness to Pay
State the price point. Where does this come from? Cross-reference with Stage 5 validation criteria. Have you validated this price with real customers?

Q3 — Unit Economics
Calculate:
- Average Contract Value (ACV) or Average Transaction Value
- Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) — estimated for your primary channel
- Gross Margin — revenue minus direct cost of delivering the product/service
- Payback Period — how long to recover the CAC from gross profit: CAC / (ACV × Gross Margin)
- LTV:CAC ratio — target 3:1 minimum for bootstrapped, 5:1+ for venture-scale at Series A

Flag immediately if:
- Gross margin is below 50% for SaaS (industry benchmark is 70–80%+)
- Payback period exceeds 18 months
- LTV:CAC is below 3:1

Q4 — Path to $1M ARR
Model the specific path: how many customers at what ACV through what channels with what team size? Show the math. This is not a growth projection — it is a capacity model.

Stage 5 Gate Score:
- Revenue model is selected and justified (1 pt)
- Price point is stated and has some validation basis (1 pt)
- Unit economics are calculated (even if estimated) (1 pt)
- No fatal unit economics flags (gross margin <50%, payback >18 months, LTV:CAC <3:1) (1 pt)
- Path to $1M ARR is modeled (1 pt)

Maximum: 5 points. Gate threshold: 4/5. Below 4: do not proceed. A business with broken unit economics cannot be fixed by GTM; fix the model first.

---

STAGE 6: GTM STRATEGY

Goal: Define a specific, executable plan for acquiring the first 100 customers.

This stage draws from the GTM Playbook framework. Cover four areas:

Area 1 — ICP (Ideal Customer Profile)
Synthesize the customer archetype identified in Stages 1–2 into a single ICP statement: specific person, behavioral trigger, watering hole, mindset. This is the targeting foundation for everything in this stage.

Area 2 — Channel Selection
Choose a primary channel and a secondary channel. For each:
- Why does this channel reach the ICP?
- What is the realistic CAC?
- What is the first experiment to run, with what budget and timeline?
Define explicitly what channels you are NOT pursuing and why.

Area 3 — 90-Day Roadmap
Map the three-phase launch plan:
- Days 1–30: Validate GTM hypothesis (10 customer conversations, 1 channel experiment, 1 strong paid signal minimum)
- Days 31–60: Build first acquisition motion (5–10 paying customers, documented process, measured CAC)
- Days 61–90: Scale what works, cut what doesn't (2–3x investment in primary channel, secondary channel initiated)

Area 4 — First 100 Customer Model
How do you get from 0 to 100 paying customers? At your ACV, what does 100 customers represent in ARR? What is the average ramp time per customer? How many customers can you handle without additional headcount?

Stage 6 Gate Score:
- ICP statement is specific (not a demographic segment) (1 pt)
- Primary channel is chosen with CAC estimate (1 pt)
- A concrete first experiment is defined (specific action, budget, timeline) (1 pt)
- 90-day roadmap has real actions (not just goals) (1 pt)
- Path to 100 customers is modeled (1 pt)

Maximum: 5 points. Gate threshold: 4/5. Below 4: the GTM strategy is too vague to execute. Return and specify the first actions before proceeding to the final stage.

---

STAGE 7: BUILD DECISION

Goal: Synthesize all evidence from Stages 1–6 into a final recommendation: Build, Pivot, or Kill.

SCORING SUMMARY
Tally the gate scores from all stages:
- Stage 1 (max 4): [score]
- Stage 2 (max 5): [score]
- Stage 3 (max 5): [score]
- Stage 4 (max 5): [score]
- Stage 5 (max 5): [score]
- Stage 6 (max 5): [score]

Total Score: [X] / 29

Score Interpretation:
- 24–29 (83–100%): Strong signal — BUILD. The fundamentals are sound. Proceed with confidence.
- 19–23 (65–82%): Mixed signal — PIVOT. Something is off. Identify the lowest-scoring stage and address it specifically before committing to build.
- Below 19 (<65%): Weak signal — KILL or major restructuring required. The idea in its current form does not have sufficient validation to justify building. Either reframe significantly or stop.

VERDICT FRAMEWORK
Beyond the score, apply these three tests before finalizing the verdict:

Test 1 — The Fatal Flaw Test
Is there any single stage where the score is 0 or 1 (regardless of total score)? A catastrophic failure in any single stage — no willingness to pay, no market, broken unit economics — is a kill signal even if other stages scored well. Flag it.

Test 2 — The Founder-Market Fit Test
Given the ICP, market, and GTM strategy: does this founding team have the domain expertise, network access, or unique capability required to execute this specific plan? A great idea with the wrong team for that idea is a pivot signal, not a build signal.

Test 3 — The Opportunity Cost Test
Given the time and capital required to validate and build this: is this the best use of this founder's next 2–3 years? This is not a formal score — it is a question to ask explicitly. Founders sometimes pass all the validation gates on an idea that is fine but not compelling enough to sustain the energy required to build a company. "Fine" is not enough. The honest answer to this question matters.

FINAL RECOMMENDATION FORMAT

Build / Pivot / Kill: [Verdict]

Score: [X] / 29 ([Percentage]%)

Rationale: [2–3 sentences summarizing the specific evidence that drives the verdict]

Strongest signal in favor: [The single most compelling piece of evidence]

Weakest signal / biggest gap: [The specific area that most undermines the case]

If Build: [The most important thing to do in the next 30 days]
If Pivot: [Specifically what to change — the customer, the problem, the model, or the GTM — and what evidence would validate the pivot]
If Kill: [What would have to be true for this to be worth revisiting in 12–18 months]

Estimated validation timeline: [How long the full pipeline took and how long a re-run of any failed stages would take]

---

Start by asking me: "Tell me your idea in two sentences — what is it, and who is it for? We'll start at Stage 1 and work through every gate."
```

## Framework & Methodology

### The Meta-Skill Concept

This is the only skill in the VibeCorp CoworkSkills collection that chains the others together. It is designed as an orchestrator: each stage activates the relevant methodology from the other skills (Problem Validation, Market Sizing, Pricing Strategy, GTM Playbook, Investor Prep) in the correct sequence, with gates that enforce the logic of that sequence.

The fundamental logic is that **validation stages are dependent, not independent.** Market sizing without problem validation produces numbers that measure the wrong universe. Pricing without market sizing has no LTV anchor. GTM without ICP definition produces channel selection that optimizes for the wrong customer. The gates exist to prevent the most common failure mode: jumping to execution before establishing the foundation that makes execution meaningful.

### Why Gates, Not Scores

Most validation frameworks produce scores. This one produces gates — binary pass/fail thresholds that must be cleared before advancing. The distinction matters because it changes the relationship between the founder and the framework.

A scoring framework with no gates can always be rationalized forward. "We scored 70% — that's pretty good, let's proceed." A gate framework forces a different question: "What specifically is missing, and what would I need to do to get it?" The gate doesn't let you proceed with the deficit acknowledged but unaddressed.

The threshold values (3/4 at Stage 1, 4/5 at Stages 2–6) are not arbitrary. They are set one point below maximum to allow for one genuine gap per stage without failing. The expectation is that no stage will score perfectly — real businesses have real uncertainties. The thresholds are set to allow progress while flagging critical deficiencies.

### The Fatal Flaw Test

The aggregate score is necessary but not sufficient. A business can score well in aggregate while having a catastrophic flaw in one area that would prevent success. A Stage 5 score of 0 (broken unit economics) cannot be compensated for by strong scores in Stages 1–4 and 6 — a business with a fundamentally unsound economic model will fail regardless of the quality of the problem validation or GTM plan.

The fatal flaw test forces a pass on the individual stage review even after a passing aggregate score. It is the safety valve that prevents a well-designed presentation from obscuring a structural problem.

### The Founder-Market Fit Test

Founder-market fit is not the same as domain expertise. It is the intersection of domain expertise, network access, and personal motivation required to navigate the specific execution challenges of a given market.

A former compliance officer building a compliance automation tool has high founder-market fit — the domain is correct, they have built-in customer access, and the problem presumably connects to personal frustration with the status quo. A generalist engineer building the same tool without that background has lower founder-market fit — the domain expertise must be hired or learned, the customer relationships must be built from scratch, and the personal connection to the problem is weaker.

This is a judgment call, not a formula, which is why it is a Test rather than a scored gate. The question is not "does this team have the right credentials?" but "is this the team most likely to execute this specific plan, in this specific market, against these specific competitors?"

### Timeline Expectations

The 4–6 week estimate for running the full pipeline is a practical target, not a minimum. Stages 1, 4, and 7 can be completed in a day with existing information. Stages 2, 3, and 5 require real-world actions that take time:

- Stage 2 (Problem Validation) requires 7–10 customer conversations. At 2 conversations per week, this takes 3–5 weeks.
- Stage 3 (Evidence Gathering) requires an honest inventory of what exists and what's missing — this can be done quickly but requires intellectual honesty to do well.
- Stage 5 (Revenue Model) requires pricing conversations with real customers. At 3–5 pricing conversations, this is 1–2 weeks if done in parallel with Stage 2.

Teams that rush through Stages 2 and 3 by using their own experience as a substitute for customer evidence are not running the pipeline — they are rationalizing a decision they've already made. The framework is designed to surface that substitution and name it as Weak or Dangerous evidence rather than letting it pass as real validation.

### Build / Pivot / Kill: What Each Verdict Means

**Build** is not "everything is perfect." It is "the evidence is sufficient to justify committing meaningful resources to this idea." There will always be open questions — the threshold is whether the remaining questions can be answered in market (through building and selling) rather than requiring more pre-build validation.

**Pivot** is the most nuanced verdict. It requires specificity: pivot what? The most common productive pivots are customer pivots (same problem, different ICP), problem pivots (same customer, more painful problem), or model pivots (same product, different revenue structure). A general "this needs work" without specificity is not a pivot recommendation — it is avoidance.

**Kill** is not a failure verdict — it is an efficiency verdict. Finding out that an idea is not worth building is the second-best outcome of the validation pipeline. The best outcome is building something that works. The third-best outcome is building something that doesn't work after investing 12–18 months. Kill at Stage 4 is better than Kill at Month 18.

## Example Usage

**User:** My idea: A mobile app that helps amateur runners track their race times and compare performance with local runners. Free app, ad-supported.

**Claude:**

Let's run this through the pipeline. I'll start at Stage 1 and hold you to the gate at each stage.

---

**STAGE 1: IDEA CAPTURE**

Q1 — What is it?
"A mobile app that helps amateur runners track their race times and compare performance with local runners."
This is reasonably specific. Let me push on one thing: "track their race times" — does this mean manual entry, GPS tracking, or integration with race results? The distinction matters because the product experience and technical requirements are very different.

Q2 — Who is it for?
"Amateur runners" is a large category. Is this for someone who runs a few 5Ks a year, a regular half-marathon runner, or someone who runs daily but never races? The difference matters for frequency of use, which drives ad revenue in an ad-supported model. Push me toward a specific archetype.

Q3 — Why now?
This is the weakest answer so far — you haven't stated why now. Running apps (Strava, Garmin, Nike Run Club) have existed for over a decade. What is new? A potential "why now" might be: the proliferation of local 5K/10K race series, the post-pandemic running boom, or a specific gap Strava hasn't addressed. What's yours?

Stage 1 Score:
- Product description is understandable: 1/1
- Real archetype: 0/1 (still too broad)
- Why now: 0/1 (not stated)
- Internal coherence: 1/1

**Stage 1 Score: 2/4 — GATE FAILED.**

Before we can proceed to Stage 2, return to Stage 1 and answer: (a) who specifically is the runner, with enough specificity to find 10 real people, and (b) what has changed in the last 1–3 years that this app addresses that didn't exist before?

*[User: "Okay — the specific person is a casual runner who does 3–8 organized races per year, age 28–45, wants to beat their PR but doesn't take it seriously enough to use Garmin or do structured training. Why now: Parkrun and local race series have exploded — there are now 1,000+ Parkrun locations in the US and hundreds of local race series, but there's no good app for tracking performance across different race formats and comparing with your local community specifically."]*

Stage 1 Revised Score:
- Product description: 1/1
- Real archetype: 1/1 (casual racer, 3–8 organized races/year)
- Why now: 1/1 (Parkrun explosion, local race series growth)
- Internal coherence: 1/1

**Stage 1 Score: 4/4 — GATE PASSED.** Proceeding to Stage 2.

---

**STAGE 2: PROBLEM VALIDATION**

Q1 — Who exactly has this problem?
The archetype is clearer now. Let me push one more level: do you personally know 3 people who fit this description? Can you introduce me (conceptually) to one of them by name, context, and relationship to the problem?

Q3 — Why is the current solution inadequate?
Strava exists. Nike Run Club exists. Garmin Connect exists. These are not nothing. What specifically breaks down for your archetype with these existing tools?

*[User: "Strava is overkill — they see it as for 'serious' runners and feel like outsiders. Nike Run Club doesn't track across different race formats. And none of them show you how you compare to other runners who run the same local Parkrun or local race series — the social comparison is national or global, not local."]*

Q5 — Willingness to pay:
You said free, ad-supported. Have you tested whether your archetype would pay anything? The willingness-to-pay question is still relevant for ad-supported models — in this case, the "payment" is attention and data. Would your archetype tolerate ads? Would they pay $2–3/month to remove them? Have you asked?

Stage 2 Score:
- Specific archetype: 1/1
- Current workaround: 1/1 (Strava, Nike Run Club — but with specific gaps named)
- Inadequacy quantified: 0/1 (the gaps are named but not quantified in time or dollars)
- Frequency: 1/1 (3–8 races per year is stated)
- Willingness to pay signal: 0/1 (ad-supported assumed, not validated)

**Stage 2 Score: 3/5 — GATE FAILED.**

Missing: (1) quantify why the lack of local comparison is actually painful — is this a mild preference or something that meaningfully reduces their engagement/enjoyment? and (2) test whether your ICP will tolerate an ad-supported experience or would pay a small monthly fee. Action: talk to 5 casual runners who use Parkrun and ask them specifically about the local comparison gap and what they'd do to have it addressed.

---

**STAGE 4: MARKET SIZING (preview)**

Even before completing the pipeline, a sizing check is useful here:

TAM: ~15 million recreational runners in the US who participate in organized races × $3/year in ad revenue per MAU = $45M TAM in ad revenue. This is a small market.

If even 10% of 15M runners use your app (1.5M MAU) and you monetize at $3/MAU/year, that's $4.5M ARR — a reasonable lifestyle business, but not a venture-scale opportunity.

**Key flag for Stage 5:** The ad-supported model has a fundamental unit economics challenge. Ad revenue per user for niche mobile apps is typically $1–5/year for casual users. To reach $1M ARR from ads, you need 200,000–1,000,000 MAU. That is a significant scale requirement before the business is viable. Consider whether a freemium model with a $2–3/month paid tier would produce better unit economics at smaller scale.

---

*[The pipeline continues through Stages 3–7, with the user returning evidence at each gate and Claude evaluating whether the threshold is met before advancing.]*

---

**FINAL RECOMMENDATION (illustrative):**

Build / Pivot / Kill: PIVOT

Score: 17/29 (59%)

Rationale: The problem is real (Stage 2 evidence is adequate), the market is underserved in the local comparison dimension, but the ad-supported revenue model produces unit economics that require scale this team cannot realistically achieve. The core insight (local community comparison for casual runners) has merit; the business model doesn't.

Strongest signal in favor: The Parkrun growth trend is a genuine behavioral shift that creates a window; the ICP is identifiable and reachable.

Biggest gap: Ad-supported revenue model at this market size is not venture-viable and is barely bootstrapped-viable. The model needs redesign before building.

Pivot recommendation: Test a freemium model ($0 for basic tracking, $3–5/month for local leaderboards and community features) against 50 ICP-matched casual runners before writing a line of code. A 5% conversion rate at $36/year ACV = $1.80 LTV per free user — still modest, but the economics improve meaningfully and the product becomes fundable as a niche vertical SaaS rather than a consumer ad play.

What would need to be true to revisit: Evidence that 10%+ of target users would pay $3–5/month, or evidence of a licensing or partnership model (race organizers paying for the community platform) that changes the unit economics.

## When to Use

- When you have a new idea and want to test it rigorously before committing time or money
- When you're at an inflection point — should you pivot, kill, or double down? — and need a structured framework to make the decision
- When an advisor or investor challenges whether the idea is ready to build and you need to work through the validation logic systematically
- As a pre-fundraising checklist — if the pipeline produces a Build verdict, you have the framework to answer every key investor question; if it produces Pivot or Kill, you know what to fix first
- For accelerator applications that ask about validation and traction — this pipeline produces the exact evidence and reasoning those applications require

## When NOT to Use

- To validate an existing business with paying customers and strong retention — you've already passed the gates; use the individual skill frameworks for specific optimization questions (pricing, GTM, investor prep)
- As a substitute for the individual skills on their own — if you only need market sizing, use the Market Sizing skill directly; the full pipeline is for idea-stage validation, not standalone analysis
- To rationalize a decision you've already made — this is the most important warning. The pipeline is most valuable when you genuinely don't know the answer. If you're running it to confirm a predetermined conclusion, the gates will be gamed and the output will be worthless. Use it with intellectual honesty or don't use it.
- For acquisition or investment analysis of an existing company — this framework is built for early-stage idea validation, not for diligence on an established business
