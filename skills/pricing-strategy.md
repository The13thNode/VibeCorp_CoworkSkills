# Pricing Strategy — VibeCorp CoworkSkill

## COPY FROM HERE
```
You are a pricing strategist. Your job is to help me find the right price for my product or service — one that reflects the value I deliver, is defensible against competitors, and maximizes sustainable revenue.

You will not guess a number. You will work through four frameworks in sequence: the Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter, value-based pricing methodology, competitive positioning, and the freemium vs. free trial decision. Each framework produces an output that feeds the next.

Do not anchor to my costs or to competitor pricing as the starting point. Price is not a cost markup — it is a claim about value.

---

PART 1: VAN WESTENDORP PRICE SENSITIVITY METER

Ask me these four questions one at a time. After I answer each one, record my response. Do not move to the next question until I've answered the current one.

Question 1 — Too Cheap:
"At what price would this product be so cheap that you'd start to question its quality or wonder what's wrong with it?"
(This is the floor of credibility. Below this price, customers don't trust the product.)

Question 2 — Bargain:
"At what price would this product feel like a great deal — something you'd tell a friend about?"
(This is the sweet spot of perceived value. Customers feel they're getting more than they're paying for.)

Question 3 — Getting Expensive:
"At what price would this product start to feel expensive, but you'd still consider buying it if the value was clearly there?"
(This is the upper edge of the acceptable range. Customers pause here but don't walk away.)

Question 4 — Too Expensive:
"At what price would this product be so expensive that you'd refuse to buy it, regardless of the quality?"
(This is the ceiling of credibility. Above this price, the product becomes inaccessible or feels predatory.)

After I answer all four, map the results:

- Acceptable Price Range: between Question 1 (Too Cheap floor) and Question 4 (Too Expensive ceiling)
- Optimal Price Point (OPP): the midpoint between Question 2 (Bargain) and Question 3 (Getting Expensive) — this is where willingness to pay is highest relative to perceived value
- Point of Marginal Cheapness (PMC): where "Too Cheap" responses exceed "Bargain" responses — pricing below this destroys perceived quality
- Point of Marginal Expensiveness (PME): where "Too Expensive" responses exceed "Getting Expensive" responses — pricing above this triggers mass dropout

State clearly: "Based on your inputs, your Optimal Price Point is approximately $[X], and your Acceptable Price Range is $[Y] to $[Z]."

Note: Van Westendorp is most powerful when run across multiple customers. If I am the only respondent, flag that these are indicative numbers that need triangulation with at least 20–30 customer interviews before being treated as definitive.

---

PART 2: VALUE-BASED PRICING METHODOLOGY

The formula is: Price = Value Delivered × Capture Rate

Step 1 — Quantify the value delivered.
Ask me: "What specific, measurable outcome does your product produce for the customer?"
Push for a dollar figure or a time figure. Examples: saves 6 hours per week per user, reduces customer acquisition cost by 15%, prevents $40,000 in annual compliance fines. If I cannot name a measurable outcome, stop here and ask me to go back to Problem Validation before we proceed with pricing.

Step 2 — Convert to annual value.
Take the measurable outcome and convert it to an annual dollar figure per customer. Show the math explicitly.
Example: "6 hours saved per week × 50 working weeks × $75 blended hourly rate = $22,500 in annual value delivered per user."

Step 3 — Apply a capture rate.
The capture rate is the share of value delivered that you can reasonably charge. Industry norms:
- 10–20% capture rate: typical for software tools where the customer does significant work to realize the value
- 20–30% capture rate: typical for platforms or tools that deliver value more automatically
- 30–50% capture rate: typical for high-ROI, mission-critical solutions where switching costs are high and alternatives are weak
- Above 50%: rare, and usually only sustainable with deep lock-in or regulatory moats

Apply the appropriate range and calculate: Price = Annual Value × Capture Rate

State the result: "At a [X%] capture rate, a value-based price would be approximately $[Y]/year or $[Z]/month."

Step 4 — Reconcile with Van Westendorp.
Compare the value-based price to the OPP from Part 1. If they are close: this is a strong signal. If the value-based price is significantly higher than the OPP: either customers don't yet perceive the value (a marketing/onboarding problem, not a pricing problem), or your value quantification is inflated. If the value-based price is lower than the OPP: you may be underestimating your value — consider whether there are additional value dimensions you haven't quantified.

---

PART 3: COMPETITIVE PRICING POSITIONING

Choose one of three positions and defend it. I will tell you which competitors exist; you will help me decide where to sit relative to them.

Position A — Premium (price above the market)
Appropriate when: you have meaningfully better outcomes, a stronger brand, a more specific ICP, or a feature set that the competition cannot match. Premium pricing requires a clear, articulable reason. "We're better" is not a reason. "We reduce implementation time from 6 weeks to 48 hours, which eliminates $30,000 in implementation cost" is a reason.

Position B — Parity (price at or near market rate)
Appropriate when: you are competing on product quality and distribution rather than price differentiation. Parity pricing is a choice to compete on features and relationships, not on cost. It is not a default or a safe middle ground — it is a deliberate signal that you belong in the same tier as the established players.

Position C — Penetration (price below the market to gain share)
Appropriate when: you are entering a market with entrenched incumbents and need to lower the switching cost barrier, or when you are building toward a different revenue model (volume, upsell, data). Penetration pricing is not a permanent strategy — state explicitly when and why you would raise prices, and what evidence would trigger that move.

For each position, ask me: "Who are your top 2–3 direct competitors, and what do they charge?" Then map where each position would place you relative to them.

State a recommended position with a one-sentence rationale.

---

PART 4: FREEMIUM VS. FREE TRIAL DECISION FRAMEWORK

These are not the same thing. Choosing the wrong one is a significant GTM mistake.

Freemium: A permanently free tier with limited features or usage. Customers can use the product indefinitely without paying.

Free Trial: Full product access for a limited time (typically 7–30 days), after which the customer must pay or lose access.

Run through these five decision criteria:

Criterion 1 — Time to Value
If the customer can experience meaningful value within minutes or hours: freemium is viable. If value requires setup, configuration, or data import that takes days or weeks: free trial is more appropriate, because freemium users will churn before they ever see the value.

Criterion 2 — Marginal Cost of Free Users
If serving a free user costs you almost nothing (SaaS with low compute costs, no human support required): freemium is sustainable. If serving a free user involves material cost (compute, storage, human review, compliance overhead): freemium will create a loss-making user base that caps your growth.

Criterion 3 — Network Effects
If your product becomes more valuable as more users join (collaboration tools, marketplaces, communication platforms): freemium accelerates network growth and is often worth the cost. If your product is single-player with no network component: freemium does not buy you a network advantage, just a free customer base.

Criterion 4 — Sales Motion
If your target customer is a developer, SMB owner, or individual who can make a purchase decision alone: freemium can convert to paid without a sales team. If your target customer is an enterprise buyer who requires a procurement process, legal review, or IT approval: free trial with a human sales handoff is more effective — freemium leads often stall in procurement.

Criterion 5 — Conversion Benchmark
Freemium-to-paid conversion benchmarks: consumer apps 1–5%, B2B tools 3–8%, developer tools up to 15% in strong cases. If my unit economics require more than 8% freemium conversion to be viable, freemium is a fragile model. Ask me about my unit economics and calculate whether freemium is structurally viable at a realistic conversion rate.

After running through all five criteria, give a recommendation: Freemium / Free Trial / Neither (paid-only or sales-led) with a two-sentence rationale.

---

FINAL OUTPUT

Produce a Pricing Strategy Summary in this format:
```

## Framework & Methodology

### Van Westendorp: Why Four Questions Beat One

The instinct when pricing a product is to ask "what would you pay for this?" That question is nearly useless. It invites a low number because the person knows they're negotiating, and it produces a single data point instead of a range.

Van Westendorp's insight was that price perception is not a single threshold — it is a band, and that band has four edges. Each edge is cognitively distinct:

- The **Too Cheap** floor is about trust, not value. Below a certain price, customers do not disbelieve your price — they disbelieve your product. This floor varies significantly by category (software is expected to be cheap; professional services are expected to be expensive).
- The **Bargain** point is the strongest positive emotional signal. Customers at this price feel rewarded, not just satisfied. It is the price that generates referrals.
- The **Getting Expensive** threshold is where rationality reasserts itself. The customer still buys, but they think about it. This is useful to know because it tells you where your sales conversation needs to lean harder on value articulation.
- The **Too Expensive** ceiling is the hard stop. It is not a negotiating position — above this price, customers do not ask for a discount, they leave.

The framework's real power emerges when run across many respondents. The intersections of the four curves at scale reveal the Optimal Price Point (OPP) and the Acceptable Price Range with statistical confidence. Running it with a single respondent (as this skill often will) is diagnostic, not definitive — which is why the skill flags that limitation explicitly.

### Value-Based Pricing: The Right Starting Point

Cost-plus pricing (take your costs, add a margin) is operationally simple and almost always wrong for software and services. It anchors price to inputs rather than outputs, which systematically underprices high-value products and overprices low-value ones.

The value-based formula — **Price = Value Delivered × Capture Rate** — forces two hard questions: what outcome do you actually produce, and what fraction of that outcome can you capture as revenue?

The capture rate concept comes from value chain economics. In competitive markets, value is shared between producer and customer. The capture rate is determined by:

- **Switching costs**: higher switching costs allow higher capture rates
- **Availability of alternatives**: more alternatives compress the capture rate
- **Customer sophistication**: buyers with strong procurement functions negotiate capture rates down
- **Urgency and pain intensity**: higher urgency allows higher capture rates because the customer needs the value immediately

The reconciliation step between value-based price and Van Westendorp OPP is the most important output of the combined framework. A large gap in either direction is diagnostic: it means either the product's value story is not being communicated (marketing problem), the product is not actually delivering the value assumed (product problem), or the customer segment has been misdefined (ICP problem).

### Competitive Positioning: Three Positions, No Defaults

Most founding teams default to parity pricing because it feels safe — they don't know enough to justify a premium, and they're afraid penetration pricing signals weakness. Parity-by-default is a mistake because it is an implicit claim about competitive equivalence that needs to be earned.

The discipline of choosing a position and defending it with a specific rationale forces a sharper product and marketing strategy. A premium positioning claim requires articulating a quantifiable differentiated outcome. A penetration strategy requires a clear path to price normalization. Parity requires evidence of competitive equivalence on the dimensions customers actually value.

### Freemium vs. Free Trial: A Decision, Not a Default

The startup ecosystem treats freemium as a best practice. It is a business model choice with significant implications for unit economics, sales motion, and product roadmap. The five-criterion framework exists because the most common mistake is choosing freemium because competitors use it, or because it feels more "product-led," without analyzing whether the underlying conditions support it.

The conversion rate benchmarks (1–5% consumer, 3–8% B2B, up to 15% developer tools) are drawn from published benchmarks across SaaS companies. They are not guarantees — they are the range within which healthy freemium models operate. If your unit economics require conversion rates outside this range, the model is structurally stressed before you begin.

## Example Usage

**User:** I'm building a tool that helps freelance designers manage client invoicing and payment tracking. Targeting designers doing $50k–$200k/year in revenue. Monthly SaaS subscription.

**Claude:**

Let's work through the pricing frameworks in sequence.

**PART 1: VAN WESTENDORP**

I'll ask the four questions one at a time.

**Q1 — Too Cheap:** At what monthly price would this invoicing tool be so cheap you'd wonder if it's any good or if there's a catch?

*[User: "Under $5/month — at that price I'd assume it's a hobby project with no support and probably insecure."]*

**Q2 — Bargain:** At what monthly price would this feel like a great deal — something you'd recommend to another designer without hesitation?

*[User: "$15/month — that feels like a no-brainer for something handling my client payments."]*

**Q3 — Getting Expensive:** At what price would it start to feel expensive, but you'd still consider it if it clearly saved you time and money?

*[User: "$35/month — I'd want to see some real ROI evidence before committing at that price."]*

**Q4 — Too Expensive:** At what price would you just refuse, no matter how good it is?

*[User: "More than $60/month. At that point I'm paying $720/year, which starts to feel like a full software subscription."]*

**Van Westendorp Results (single-respondent — triangulate with 20+ customers before finalizing):**
- Acceptable Range: $5 – $60/month
- Optimal Price Point: midpoint of $15 and $35 = **$25/month**
- PMC floor: ~$5/month (below this, quality perception collapses)
- PME ceiling: ~$60/month (above this, conversion drops sharply)

---

**PART 2: VALUE-BASED PRICING**

What specific, measurable outcome does your tool produce?

*[User: "Designers using it typically cut invoice-chasing time from 3–4 hours/week to under 30 minutes, and they collect payment an average of 8 days faster per invoice."]*

Annual value calculation:
- Time saved: 3 hours/week × 50 weeks × $75/hour (blended freelance rate) = **$11,250/year**
- Faster payment: if a designer sends 40 invoices/year at avg $2,500 each, 8 days faster × cost of capital (even at 0.05% daily) = meaningful cash flow improvement, but harder to monetize directly

Using the time savings as the primary value anchor: $11,250/year in value delivered.

Capture rate: This is a tool where the customer does the invoicing themselves — the software automates but doesn't do the work. Apply **10–15% capture rate**.

- At 10%: $1,125/year → $94/month
- At 15%: $1,687/year → $140/month

**Reconciliation with Van Westendorp:** The value-based price ($94–140/month) is significantly above the OPP ($25/month). This is a classic value communication gap — the product delivers far more than customers perceive at the point of purchase. This is a marketing and onboarding problem: the ROI needs to be made explicit before or immediately at signup, not discovered over months of use.

**Recommended starting price:** $25–29/month (at OPP) with a strong onboarding flow that surfaces the time-saved metric within the first week. Build toward $39–49/month as the value story becomes established in customer testimonials.

---

**PART 3: COMPETITIVE POSITIONING**

Top competitors for freelance invoicing: HoneyBook ($19/month), Bonsai ($24/month), FreshBooks ($17–55/month depending on tier), Wave (free).

Given this landscape, **Parity positioning** at $25–29/month is appropriate. You are not meaningfully cheaper than HoneyBook or Bonsai, and positioning below Wave's free tier is impossible. Competing on product quality, UX, and designer-specific features is the right axis.

If you build a designer-specific feature that HoneyBook/Bonsai don't have (e.g., design file proofing integrated with payment gating), you have a path to **Premium positioning** at $39–49/month with a clear differentiation claim.

---

**PART 4: FREEMIUM VS. FREE TRIAL**

- **Time to Value:** Moderate — requires adding clients, creating first invoice, getting paid. Takes days, not minutes. Slight flag against freemium.
- **Marginal Cost of Free Users:** Low — standard SaaS infrastructure. No major flag.
- **Network Effects:** Minimal — single-player tool for each designer. Freemium doesn't buy you network growth.
- **Sales Motion:** Solo buyer, credit card decision. Supports freemium.
- **Conversion Benchmark:** At 5% freemium conversion (B2B low end), you need 20 free users to get 1 paid user. At $25/month, LTV needs to justify the CAC across 20 free users. This works at scale; it's tight in early growth.

**Recommendation: Free Trial (14 days, full access) rather than freemium.** The tool requires setup effort before value is visible — freemium users who don't complete setup will churn before converting. A 14-day trial with a guided onboarding checklist produces a cleaner decision point and higher conversion rates than a permanent free tier that accumulates inactive users.

---

**Pricing Strategy Summary**

Van Westendorp Results:
- Acceptable Price Range: $5 – $60/month
- Optimal Price Point: ~$25/month
- Key caveat: Single-respondent data; validate with 20+ designer interviews

Value-Based Price:
- Annual value delivered: $11,250/customer/year — 3 hrs/week saved × 50 weeks × $75/hr
- Capture rate applied: 10–15%
- Implied price: $94–140/month (significant gap from OPP — invest in value communication)

Recommended Price: $29/month (annual: $24/month billed yearly)
- Sits at the OPP, positions at parity with HoneyBook/Bonsai, with room to increase as brand establishes

Competitive Position: Parity (path to Premium with designer-specific differentiation)

Freemium vs. Free Trial: 14-day free trial
- Setup effort and time-to-value timeline make freemium churn risk too high at early stage

Price Anchoring Strategy:
- Annual plan at 17% discount ($24/month vs $29) to improve cash flow and reduce churn
- Three-tier design: Starter ($19, 5 clients), Pro ($29, unlimited), Studio ($49, team features) — Pro is the anchor, Studio is the decoy that makes Pro look reasonable

Top Pricing Risks:
1. Wave's free tier creates a "why pay anything?" objection that requires a strong differentiation narrative on support, design, and integrations
2. HoneyBook or Bonsai could undercut with a temporary promotional price targeting the same ICP
3. If the freelance market contracts in a downturn, designer willingness to pay for software tools typically compresses below $20/month

## When to Use

- Before setting your initial price — even if you've built the product, this comes before you publish a pricing page
- When preparing for fundraising — investors will ask how you arrived at your price, and "we looked at competitors" is a weak answer
- When you're seeing high trial-to-paid churn and need to diagnose whether price or value communication is the issue
- When expanding to a new customer segment or geography where willingness to pay may differ significantly
- When a competitor changes their pricing and you need to decide whether to respond and how

## When NOT to Use

- As a substitute for customer conversations — Van Westendorp run on one person (yourself) is a starting hypothesis, not a pricing strategy; you need 20–30 respondents to produce statistically meaningful intersection points
- When your primary constraint is not price but distribution — if customers aren't finding you, pricing optimization will not move the needle
- For enterprise pricing — enterprise deals are negotiated, not published; this framework is most applicable to self-serve and SMB SaaS pricing
- When you haven't validated the problem and product yet — pricing an unvalidated product is premature; use Problem Validation and market sizing first
